Bioluminescence For Dummies

☰

For my EPQ I am designing a website and a game with the theme of Bioluminecence. First Draft.

Bioluminescence is a natural chemical process which allows living things to produce light in their body. It occurs in many living creatures, some of which you may be very familiar with. This is a bioluminescent octopus, or (if you’re fancy) Stauroteuthis Syrtensis . My personal favourite name for it, however, is the “glowing sucker octopus”. They are a small species of octopus which live in the depths of the North Atlantic Ocean, which is the ocean between North America and Europe. What makes these octopus really special is the fact that they are one of two types of octopuses which can glow, and because they live so deep, we know relatively little about them!

The Glowing Sucker Octopus is actually very small, its main body or “mantle” is only around 2-4 inches which is around the size of your finger! And its full body including its tentacles is only around 14 inches or 35cm, that’s about the size of a ruler. Despite their small size they are incredibly intelligent and can survive in some very harsh conditions. They can live almost 2.5 miles deep in the ocean at temperatures around 3 degrees C! Imagine how tall the Eiffel tower is, now imagine 13 times that! That’s how deep they live.

Scientists don’t know for sure why they glow in the dark. Most other octopuses don’t have the ability to produce bioluminescence, they instead have different uses for their suckers. However, their main ideas are that the ability is used to lure in prey or scare off predators.

Now you've had a little introduction to Bioluminescence, let me tell you why I find it so facinating:

  1. It shows how something almost magical can be made through evolution and science
  2. The research on it is groundbreaking and very new.
  3. Scientists theorise that the ability to produce bioluminescence allowed many organisms to move deeper and deeper into the ocean.
  4. The variety of animals that produce bioluminescence is huge, from microscopic algae to fireflies to deep sea jellyfish.
  5. Finally, it's just really pretty!
